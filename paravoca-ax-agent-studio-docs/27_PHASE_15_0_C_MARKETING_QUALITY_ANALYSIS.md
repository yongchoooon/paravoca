- headline/subheadline/first section repeat product fields: observed in 22 product(s).
- claim limits include internal avoid/negative constraints: observed in 22 product(s).
- confirmation-heavy language dominates FAQ/disclaimer: observed in 21 product(s).

- Sales copy often uses the product title as `headline`, the one-liner as `subheadline`, and then repeats both in the first section. This makes the output feel mechanically assembled.
- FAQ repeatedly asks about operating hours, weather, foreign-language support, and price, then answers with “확인 필요” or “제공되지 않았습니다”. These are risk notes, not buyer-oriented FAQs.
- SNS posts frequently rely on generic emotional promises such as “잊지 못할”, “특별한”, “힐링”, “낭만”, “인생샷”, and broad hashtags rather than product-specific hooks.
- Claim limits often include internal/request-derived constraints such as “요청 avoid 기준” or “운영시간 확정”, which are useful guardrails but not marketable claims.

### Product Naming Improvement Criteria

- Product names should combine a concrete place/event anchor with a buyer-facing experience promise. Example shape: `[specific place/event] + [distinct activity/benefit]`, not only “힐링”, “낭만”, or “숨겨진 오아시스”.
- Avoid using the same abstract emotional words across products in one run. Each product should have its own angle such as night photography, low-crowd walkability, family festival, island geology, food crawl, or historical storytelling.
- If the request targets foreigners, names should make the travel context legible without only adding “외국인 대상”. Use accessibility, language support, cultural entry point, transport ease, or first-time visitor framing when evidence allows it.
- Names should not imply unsupported guarantees such as “인생샷”, “완벽한 휴식”, “최고의 명소”, or always-available experiences unless the supporting evidence is explicit.

### Sales Copy / FAQ / SNS Prompt Improvement Direction

Sales copy:
- Require a non-repeating structure: hook, why this product exists, who it is for, what the customer does, what makes it different, and what must be confirmed before booking.
- Separate sales value from operational caveats. Caveats should not dominate the headline/subheadline/first section.
- Require concrete nouns from evidence: venue names, event dates, route names, terrain, district, food/culture element, or visual cue.

FAQ:
- Include buyer questions, not only risk disclaimers: who it is best for, how much walking is involved, best time window, what to prepare, language/accessibility considerations, weather alternatives, and booking/confirmation needs.
- If evidence is missing, answer with a useful next action instead of repeating “별도 확인 필요”.

SNS:
- Generate platform-shaped copy: one concise hook, one concrete scene, one reason to save/share, and hashtags that are specific to the product.
- Avoid repeated generic hooks. Each product in a run should have a different creative angle and vocabulary.
- For foreigner-targeted products, include bilingual or internationally legible hooks only when appropriate, but do not replace substance with English hashtags alone.

Claims:
- Split claims into `usable_selling_points`, `confirmation_needed`, and `do_not_claim`.
- Do not expose “요청 avoid 기준” as a marketing claim. It belongs in QA/guardrail metadata.
- Preserve evidence safety while still surfacing positive facts that can be used in copy.

### Minimum Output Depth Criteria

- Product title: one specific, non-generic title plus optional short internal rationale.
- One-liner: one concrete sentence that includes place/activity/customer value, not just mood words.
- Sales copy: at least 4 sections, each with a distinct role: hook, experience, differentiation, practical confirmation.
- FAQ: at least 6 questions, with at least 3 buyer-value questions and at most 3 pure risk/confirmation questions.
- SNS: at least 3 posts with different angles, not three variations of the same “special night/healing” sentence.
- Claims: at least 3 usable selling points when evidence allows, plus separate confirmation and prohibited claims.
- Foreigner-targeted request: at least 2 concrete foreigner-context details when evidence allows, such as language friction, public transit, cultural explanation, first-time visitor suitability, or payment/booking uncertainty.

### Evidence-safe but Marketable Copy Principles

- Use evidence as a source of specificity, not only as a reason to add disclaimers.
- Market the experience that is supported by evidence; move unsupported operational facts into confirmation notes.
- Replace guarantees with bounded invitations: “즐길 수 있습니다” is safer than “완벽한 경험을 선사합니다”, but it still needs a concrete scene.
- Make every product in the same run sell a different reason to choose it.
- Keep disclaimers short, localized, and separate from persuasive copy.
- If evidence is weak, say what can be safely promoted and what must be verified, instead of turning the whole output into administrative caution.
